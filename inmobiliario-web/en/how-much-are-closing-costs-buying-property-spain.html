How Much Are Closing Costs When Buying Property in Spain: Complete Breakdown 2024

Many foreign buyers are shocked to discover that closing costs in Spain can add 10-15% to their property purchase price. This comprehensive guide breaks down every expense you'll face, with real figures and insider tips to avoid costly surprises.

Publicidad

When I helped my first British client buy an apartment in Marbella back in 2014, they nearly walked away from the deal at the last minute. Why? Because nobody had properly explained how much are closing costs when buying property in Spain. They had budgeted €250,000 for their dream apartment but hadn't anticipated the additional €30,000+ in closing costs. This scenario plays out far too often, and it's entirely avoidable with proper planning.

Understanding closing costs in Spain is crucial because they represent a significant portion of your total investment—typically between 10% and 15% of the property's purchase price. Unlike some countries where closing costs might be 2-3%, Spain's system includes substantial taxes, mandatory fees, and professional services that can't be avoided. The exact amount depends on whether you're buying new or resale property, the region you're purchasing in, and your residency status.

In this comprehensive guide, I'll break down every single cost you'll encounter when buying property in Spain, share real examples from transactions I've handled, and give you insider tips that can save you thousands of euros. Let's ensure you're fully prepared financially for your Spanish property purchase.

Understanding the Main Tax Component: ITP vs IVA

The largest single closing cost you'll face is taxation, but which tax you pay depends entirely on whether you're buying a resale property or a new construction. This distinction is critical and affects your total costs significantly.

Transfer Tax (ITP) for Resale Properties

When buying a resale property (segunda mano), you'll pay Impuesto de Transmisiones Patrimoniales (ITP), which varies by autonomous community. In my experience helping clients across Spain, here are the current rates in popular regions:

  • Andalusia: 7% for properties under €400,000, rising to 10% above €700,000
  • Valencia: 10% standard rate
  • Catalonia: 10% for properties under €500,000, 11% up to €1 million
  • Madrid: 6% standard rate
  • Balearic Islands: 8-11.5% on a sliding scale

For example, if you're buying a €300,000 resale villa in Alicante (Valencia region), your ITP would be €30,000. This is paid directly to the regional tax authority within 30 days of signing the deed, though your lawyer typically handles this process.

VAT and Stamp Duty for New Properties

New properties (obra nueva) purchased directly from developers are subject to IVA (VAT) at 10% for residential properties. Additionally, you must pay Actos Jurídicos Documentados (AJD), commonly called stamp duty, which ranges from 0.5% to 1.5% depending on the region.

Taking that same €300,000 purchase price for a new apartment in Valencia, you'd pay €30,000 in IVA plus approximately €4,500 in stamp duty (1.5% in Valencia), totaling €34,500—noticeably more than the resale property tax.

Professional Fees You Cannot Avoid

Beyond taxes, several professional services are essential when buying property in Spain. These fees are sometimes negotiable but generally fall within standard ranges.

Legal Fees (Abogado)

Hiring a lawyer is not legally required in Spain, but I've witnessed countless problems when buyers skip this step. A competent property lawyer charges 1% to 1.5% of the purchase price plus IVA (21%). For a €300,000 property, expect to pay €3,000-€4,500 plus €630-€945 in IVA, totaling approximately €3,630-€5,445.

Your lawyer will conduct title searches, verify the property is free from debts and encumbrances, review contracts, attend the signing, and register the property in your name. This is money exceptionally well spent.

Notary Fees (Notario)

The notary is a public official who witnesses and legalizes the property deed. Fees are regulated by the government and based on property value, typically ranging from €600 to €1,200 for most residential properties. For a €300,000 property, expect around €850. These fees are non-negotiable as they're set by official scales.

Land Registry Fees (Registro de la Propiedad)

Registering your property with the Land Registry is essential to protect your ownership rights. Fees are also government-regulated and typically cost €400 to €800 depending on the property value. For our €300,000 example, budget approximately €500.

Gestor or Property Management Service

Many lawyers include gestor services, but if hired separately, expect to pay €300-€600 for handling administrative paperwork, obtaining your NIE number, and setting up utilities.

Additional Costs That Add Up Quickly

Beyond the major expenses, several smaller costs can catch buyers off guard. From my decade of experience, these are the ones most frequently overlooked.

Bank Fees and Mortgage Costs

If you're financing your purchase, Spanish banks charge various fees that can add 2-3% of the mortgage amount:

  • Mortgage arrangement fee: 0.5-1% of loan amount
  • Valuation fee: €300-€600
  • Bank's notary and registry fees: €800-€1,200
  • Mortgage deed stamp duty: 0.5-1.5% depending on region
  • Bank administration fee: €200-€400

For a €200,000 mortgage, total bank-related costs could reach €5,000-€7,000. Since 2019, following a Supreme Court ruling, buyers are no longer responsible for all mortgage establishment costs—banks now cover some expenses—but significant costs remain.

Property Survey and Surveys

While not mandatory, I strongly recommend a professional property survey, especially for older properties. A basic survey costs €300-€600, while a comprehensive structural survey runs €800-€1,500. I've seen clients discover €20,000 worth of structural problems through a €500 survey, making it one of the best investments you can make.

Insurance

Home insurance is mandatory if you're taking a mortgage and highly advisable even if paying cash. Annual premiums typically range from €200-€600 depending on property size, location, and coverage level. Most insurers require payment upfront at completion.

Community Fees and Property Tax Prorations

At completion, you'll need to reimburse the seller for any prepaid community fees and property taxes (IBI) covering the period after you take ownership. For a property with €100 monthly community fees and €800 annual IBI, purchased mid-year, expect to pay around €1,000 in prorations.

Real Cost Example: Putting It All Together

Let me show you two real-world scenarios based on actual transactions I've handled, so you can see exactly what to budget.

Scenario 1: Resale Apartment in Valencia (€300,000)

  • Purchase price: €300,000
  • Transfer Tax (ITP) 10%: €30,000
  • Legal fees (1.2% + IVA): €4,356
  • Notary fees: €850
  • Land Registry: €500
  • Property survey: €450
  • Home insurance (first year): €350
  • Gestor services: €400
  • Prorations: €800
  • Total closing costs: €37,706 (12.6% of purchase price)

Scenario 2: New Villa in Marbella (€500,000)

  • Purchase price: €500,000
  • IVA (10%): €50,000
  • Stamp Duty AJD (1.2%): €6,000
  • Legal fees (1% + IVA): €6,050
  • Notary fees: €1,200
  • Land Registry: €750
  • Home insurance: €500
  • Gestor services: €450
  • Total closing costs: €64,950 (13% of purchase price)

These examples demonstrate why the standard advice is to budget an additional 10-15% beyond your purchase price for all closing costs when buying property in Spain.

Errores Comunes Que Debes Evitar

Having guided hundreds of transactions, I've seen these mistakes repeatedly—and they can cost you dearly:

  • Not budgeting for closing costs upfront: This is the number one mistake. Buyers focus entirely on the purchase price and scramble to find an extra 10-15% at the last minute, sometimes losing their deposit or dream property.
  • Accepting the developer's recommended lawyer: While not always problematic, the lawyer should represent YOUR interests exclusively. I've seen developer-recommended lawyers overlook issues that cost buyers thousands in corrections later.
  • Skipping the independent property survey: The bank valuation is for the lender's protection, not yours. Only an independent survey will identify structural problems, illegal extensions, or planning issues.
  • Not verifying the property's legal status: Ensure your lawyer confirms the property has a Licencia de Primera Ocupación (first occupation license) for new builds or a Cédula de Habitabilidad (habitation certificate) showing it's legally habitable.
  • Paying cash without proper documentation: All payments should be made via bank transfer with clear paper trails. Cash payments offer no protection and can raise money laundering concerns.
  • Forgetting about non-resident taxes: If you're a non-resident, remember you'll face annual obligations including non-resident income tax (even on an unused property) and wealth tax if applicable. Budget for ongoing costs, not just purchase expenses.
  • Not obtaining multiple quotes for legal services: Lawyer fees can vary significantly. I've seen quotes ranging from €3,000 to €8,000 for the same €300,000 property. Get at least three quotes, but don't choose solely on price—experience with foreign buyers is invaluable.

Preguntas Frecuentes (FAQ)

Can closing costs be included in the mortgage?

Generally, no. Spanish banks typically lend up to 60-70% of the property value for non-residents (80% for residents), but this percentage applies only to the purchase price, not closing costs. You'll need to pay the 10-15% closing costs from your own funds. Some banks may finance certain mortgage-related costs, but taxes and professional fees must be paid separately.

Are closing costs different for non-residents versus residents?

The closing costs themselves are identical, but non-residents face additional challenges. Mortgage access is more restricted (60-70% LTV vs 80% for residents), and non-residents may pay slightly higher legal fees due to additional documentation requirements. Additionally, non-residents face ongoing annual tax obligations that residents don't, including non-resident income tax on imputed rental income.

When exactly do I need to pay the closing costs?

Payment timing varies by expense. The deposit (typically 10% of purchase price) is paid when signing the private purchase contract (contrato de arras). The remaining purchase price plus most closing costs are due at the notary signing (escritura). Transfer tax or IVA must be paid within 30 business days after the deed is signed. Your lawyer will provide a detailed payment schedule, and funds should be transferred to Spain at least one week before completion.

Can I negotiate any of the closing costs?

Some costs are negotiable while others are fixed by law. Notary and Land Registry fees are government-regulated and non-negotiable. Legal fees can often be negotiated, especially on higher-value properties—I've successfully negotiated reductions from 1.5% to 1% on properties above €500,000. Survey costs are market-based and can be shopped around. Taxes cannot be negotiated, though your lawyer can advise on legitimate tax optimization strategies.

What happens if I don't register the property at the Land Registry?

While technically possible to skip registration to save €400-€800, this is extremely unwise. Without Land Registry registration, you cannot prove ownership against third parties, the property could be sold again to someone else, and existing debts or mortgages might not be discoverable. Your ownership isn't fully protected until registered. This small saving exposes you to catastrophic risk—I've seen unregistered buyers lose their entire investment when sellers disappeared or double-sold properties.

Conclusion: Plan Ahead for a Smooth Purchase

Understanding exactly how much are closing costs when buying property in Spain is fundamental to successful property ownership. The key takeaway from my decade of experience is simple: always budget for an additional 10-15% beyond the purchase price to cover all closing costs comfortably.

For resale properties, your main costs will be the 6-10% transfer tax plus professional fees. For new builds, expect 10% IVA, 0.5-1.5% stamp duty, and similar professional fees. Don't forget mortgage-related costs if financing, and always include smaller expenses like insurance, surveys, and prorations in your calculations.

The buyers who succeed in Spain are those who plan comprehensively from the start. Get your finances in order early, obtain quotes from multiple professionals, and work with experienced lawyers who specialize in helping foreign buyers. The small investment in proper professional guidance will save you multiples of that cost in avoided problems.

With proper planning and professional guidance, your Spanish property purchase can be straightforward and successful.
