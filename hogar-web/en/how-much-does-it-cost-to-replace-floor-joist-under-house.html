How Much Does It Cost to Replace a Floor Joist Under a House: Complete Price Breakdown

Floor joist replacement costs between $100-$300 per joist on average, but the total project can range from $1,000 to $10,000+ depending on accessibility and damage extent. This guide breaks down every cost factor based on real-world experience to help you budget accurately.

Publicidad

When you notice sagging floors, squeaking sounds, or visible damage in your crawl space, you're likely facing a floor joist problem that won't fix itself. Understanding how much it costs to replace a floor joist under a house is crucial before you start this essential structural repair, as the price can vary dramatically based on factors most homeowners never consider until they're deep into the project.

I've replaced dozens of floor joists over my career, and I can tell you that the advertised "$150 per joist" you see online rarely tells the whole story. Between access issues, hidden damage, code requirements, and the domino effect of related repairs, most homeowners end up spending significantly more than their initial estimates. In this comprehensive guide, I'll break down the real costs you'll face, share insider knowledge about what drives prices up, and help you budget realistically for this critical home repair.

Whether you're dealing with rot from moisture damage, termite destruction, or structural settling, replacing floor joists is non-negotiable work that protects your home's value and safety. Let's dive into exactly what you'll pay and why.

Understanding Floor Joist Replacement Costs: The Basic Breakdown

The cost to replace a floor joist under your house typically ranges from $100 to $300 per individual joist for straightforward replacements. However, most projects involve multiple joists and additional complications that push total costs to $1,000 to $10,000 or more. Here's what influences these numbers:

Material costs for dimensional lumber (the most common joist material) run between $15-$50 per joist depending on size and wood species. A standard 2x10x16 treated lumber joist costs around $30-$40 at current lumber prices, while engineered lumber like LVL (Laminated Veneer Lumber) can cost $60-$100 per joist but offers superior strength and consistency.

Labor costs represent the bulk of your expense, typically ranging from $70-$90 per hour for skilled contractors. A single joist replacement in an accessible crawl space might take 3-4 hours, but difficult access or extensive damage can extend this to a full day or more per joist. Most contractors charge a project minimum of $500-$800 even for single joist work because of the setup and expertise required.

Factors That Dramatically Affect Your Total Cost

  • Accessibility: Crawl spaces under 18 inches tall can double labor costs due to difficulty working in confined spaces
  • Number of joists: Replacing 5-10 joists often reduces per-joist costs by 20-30% compared to single replacements
  • Structural support needs: Temporary support systems and house jacking add $300-$1,000 to projects
  • Related damage: Subfloor replacement, rim joist repairs, or sill plate work can add $500-$3,000
  • Permits and inspections: Required in most areas, adding $100-$400 to project costs

Material Costs and Options for Floor Joist Replacement

Choosing the right material for your floor joist replacement affects both immediate costs and long-term durability. From my experience, spending a bit more on quality materials often saves thousands in future repairs.

Dimensional Lumber Options

Pressure-treated lumber is the standard choice for floor joists, costing $25-$45 per joist for common sizes (2x8, 2x10, 2x12). This material resists moisture and insect damage, making it ideal for crawl spaces and basements. I always recommend ground-contact rated lumber (0.60 retention level) even if it's not technically required—the extra $5-$10 per joist is cheap insurance.

Untreated kiln-dried lumber costs $20-$35 per joist and works fine in completely dry environments, but I've seen too many homeowners regret this choice when unexpected moisture appears. The 20-30% cost savings disappears quickly when you're replacing rotted joists again in 10 years.

Engineered Lumber Alternatives

Engineered I-joists and LVL beams cost more upfront ($60-$120 per joist) but offer significant advantages in certain situations. These products are stronger, straighter, and more stable than dimensional lumber. When you're replacing joists under a bathroom or kitchen where loads are higher, the extra strength prevents future sagging issues.

I used engineered joists on a project last year where the homeowner had limited access—the lighter weight made installation possible where full-dimension lumber would have been nearly impossible to maneuver. The $400 premium on materials saved an estimated $1,500 in additional labor costs for creating better access.

Additional Materials You'll Need

  • Joist hangers: $3-$8 each (code-required in most areas)
  • Construction adhesive: $6-$10 per tube (need 2-3 tubes for typical project)
  • Galvanized nails and structural screws: $25-$40 per project
  • Temporary support posts: $15-$30 each (rental or purchase)
  • Moisture barrier/vapor barrier: $50-$150 if crawl space needs improvement

Labor Costs: What Professional Installation Really Costs

Labor represents 60-75% of your total floor joist replacement cost, and this is where prices vary most dramatically between contractors and regions. In my experience working across multiple states, urban areas like Seattle, Boston, or San Francisco see rates of $90-$120 per hour, while rural regions might charge $60-$80 per hour.

A straightforward single joist replacement takes a skilled contractor about 3-4 hours, resulting in $210-$360 in labor costs. However, "straightforward" is rarer than you'd think. Most jobs I encounter involve complications that extend the timeline.

What Increases Labor Time and Costs

Limited crawl space height is the biggest labor multiplier I encounter. Working in spaces under 24 inches tall reduces productivity by 50% or more. What should be a 4-hour job becomes an 8-hour ordeal of awkward positions and frequent breaks. Some contractors add a 50-100% surcharge for confined space work, and it's justified.

Removing old joists is often more time-consuming than installing new ones. Joists attached with nails from above through the subfloor require careful cutting to avoid damaging good wood. I budget 1-2 hours per joist just for removal when dealing with properly installed original construction.

Setting temporary supports before joist removal adds 1-3 hours to projects. This crucial step prevents floor collapse and cracking of finished surfaces above. I use adjustable steel posts every 4-6 feet along the affected area, and proper setup cannot be rushed.

Project Complexity and Total Labor Hours

For a typical project replacing 3-5 joists in a moderately accessible crawl space, expect 12-20 total labor hours, translating to $960-$1,800 in labor costs at average rates. This includes assessment, temporary support installation, removal, installation, and cleanup.

Extensive projects involving 10+ joists with subfloor damage can require 40-60 hours or more, especially if the contractor needs to address underlying moisture problems, insect damage, or foundation issues discovered during the work.

Hidden Costs and Related Repairs You Should Expect

This section separates realistic budgets from wishful thinking. In over a decade of structural repair work, I've rarely completed a floor joist replacement without encountering at least one unexpected cost.

Subfloor Damage

If your joists need replacement, there's a 70% chance the subfloor above has damage too. Replacing sections of subfloor adds $3-$7 per square foot in materials and labor. A typical bathroom floor might need 80-120 square feet replaced, adding $240-$840 to your project. I always warn clients to budget an extra $500-$1,000 for potential subfloor work.

Moisture Remediation

Joist rot almost always indicates moisture problems that need addressing. Installing proper vapor barriers costs $0.50-$2.00 per square foot. A 1,000 square foot crawl space needs $500-$2,000 for vapor barrier installation. Add another $300-$800 for improved ventilation or dehumidification systems. Skipping this work means your new joists will rot too—I've seen it happen in as little as 3-5 years.

Pest Treatment and Prevention

If termites or carpenter ants caused the joist damage, professional treatment costs $500-$2,500 depending on infestation severity. This isn't optional—replacing joists without treating pests is throwing money away. I once worked on a house where the homeowner skipped treatment to save $800, and two years later needed another $6,000 in repairs.

Additional Structural Issues

  • Rim joist replacement: $200-$400 per section (often damaged when floor joists are)
  • Sill plate replacement: $15-$30 per linear foot plus labor
  • Foundation repairs: $500-$5,000+ if settlement caused the joist failure
  • Floor leveling compound: $150-$600 for restoring level finished floors above
  • Finished floor replacement: $3-$12 per square foot if tiles crack or hardwood damage occurs

Errores Comunes Que Debes Evitar

After years of fixing other people's mistakes and learning from my own early career errors, these are the costly mistakes I see homeowners and inexperienced contractors make repeatedly:

  • Replacing joists without addressing the underlying cause: This is the number one mistake. If moisture, pests, or foundation issues caused the damage, they'll destroy your new joists too. I've seen homeowners spend $3,000 on joist replacement only to need the same work again in 3-5 years because they didn't fix a $200 drainage problem.
  • Skipping temporary support systems: Some DIYers and budget contractors try to replace joists one at a time without proper temporary supports. This causes floor sagging, drywall cracks, and in extreme cases, structural failure. Always support the floor before removing any joist.
  • Using undersized replacement joists: Matching the old joist size isn't always code-compliant. Building codes have evolved, and span tables might require larger joists than originally installed. I've seen inspectors red-tag work requiring expensive do-overs when contractors didn't verify current code requirements.
  • Ignoring joist hangers or proper connection methods: Face-nailing joists to beams or rim joists is not code-compliant in most areas. Proper joist hangers cost $5-$8 each but provide crucial structural integrity. Skipping them saves $50 and creates a liability nightmare.
  • Choosing the cheapest contractor without vetting experience: Floor joist work is structural carpentry requiring specific skills. The low-bid contractor who's primarily a remodeler or handyman may lack crucial knowledge about beam sizing, temporary shoring, and code compliance. I've repaired numerous botched joist replacements that cost homeowners double the original savings.
  • Not obtaining required permits: Many homeowners try to avoid the $150-$400 permit cost, but unpermitted structural work creates serious problems when selling your house. It can also void insurance coverage if problems occur. Always permit structural work.
  • Attempting DIY work beyond skill level: I'm all for DIY projects, but floor joists support your entire house. Improper installation causes bouncy floors, cracked walls, and potentially dangerous structural failures. Unless you have carpentry experience and understand load-bearing calculations, hire a professional for this work.

Preguntas Frecuentes

Can I replace just one floor joist or do I need to do multiple at once?

You can replace individual joists, but there's often cost efficiency in addressing multiple joists simultaneously. The contractor setup costs (temporary supports, access prep, permits) are similar whether doing one joist or five, so the per-joist cost drops significantly for multiple replacements. However, if only one joist is damaged and others are sound, replacing just that one is perfectly acceptable. I always inspect adjacent joists carefully though—damage rarely affects just one joist in isolation.

How long does floor joist replacement take?

A single joist replacement in an accessible crawl space takes 4-6 hours for experienced contractors. Multiple joists (3-5) typically require 1-2 full days. Extensive projects with 10+ joists, difficult access, or related repairs can take 3-5 days or more. Weather affects crawl space work too—heavy rain can delay projects since you don't want to work in standing water or on saturated ground.

Will I need to move out during floor joist replacement?

Most floor joist replacements don't require moving out. The work happens underneath the house in the crawl space or basement. You'll hear noise and may experience some vibration, and contractors will need access to that area. If extensive subfloor replacement is needed requiring removal of finished flooring above, you might want to stay elsewhere for 2-3 days, especially if bathrooms or kitchens are affected.

How can I tell if my floor joists need replacement or just sistering?

Sistering (attaching a new joist alongside a damaged one) works when the existing joist has less than 25% section loss and isn't actively rotting. If the joist has significant rot, insect damage affecting more than 25% of the cross-section, or has cracked completely, full replacement is necessary. I use an awl to probe suspect wood—if it sinks in easily or wood crumbles, that joist needs replacement, not sistering. When in doubt, a structural engineer inspection costs $300-$500 and provides definitive answers.

Does homeowners insurance cover floor joist replacement?

Insurance coverage depends entirely on the cause of damage. Sudden events like burst pipes or covered storm damage are typically covered, minus your deductible. Gradual deterioration from age, poor maintenance, moisture issues, or pest damage is almost never covered. I always recommend homeowners file a claim before starting work if there's any possibility of coverage. One client recovered $8,000 when we documented that a plumbing leak (not general rot) caused their joist damage.

Conclusion: Budgeting Realistically for Floor Joist Replacement

Understanding how much it costs to replace a floor joist under a house requires looking beyond simple per-joist prices. While individual joists might cost $100-$300 to replace in ideal conditions, real-world projects typically run $2,500-$7,000 for addressing multiple joists with related issues in average homes. Complex situations with extensive damage, difficult access, or significant related repairs can exceed $10,000.

My best advice after handling countless joist replacement projects: get at least three detailed quotes from licensed contractors with structural experience, budget 20-30% above the highest quote for contingencies, and never choose a contractor based solely on price. Floor joists are critical structural components, and proper replacement requires expertise, quality materials, and attention to code requirements.

If you're facing floor joist issues, address them promptly. The longer you wait, the more extensive and expensive the damage becomes. What starts as a $2,000 project can easily become a $10,000+ problem after another year of deterioration. Get a professional assessment soon, understand the full scope of work needed including underlying causes, and budget realistically for this essential home repair. Your home's structural integrity and your family's safety depend on it.
